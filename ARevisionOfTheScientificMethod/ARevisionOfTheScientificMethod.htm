A Revision of the Scientific Method

Summary

A revision of the standard scientific method is proposed that explicitly handles untestable hypotheses.
Building on my previous essay, The Arrow of Time, it is assumed that there are two types of time: clock time (the time used in physics) and perceived time.
Arguments are presented showing that
  • clock time is finite, but its beginning is intrinsically undefinable
  • perceived time is also finite but it has no beginning
  • mathematical physicists can and should ignore infinities
  • relativity and quantum theory can be reconciled
  • all hypotheses, not just scientific ones, can be included in the same methodology.
Feedback describing any serious mistakes, or details that could be improved, would be very welcome. My email address can be found here.

The revision

The current version of the scientific method fails to deal cleanly with hypotheses that can't be tested.
The following pseudo-code includes a check for testability, and returns "notTestable" (neither "refuted" nor "notRefuted") if the check fails: 
scientificMethod(observer, hypothesis)
{
   if(observer.canTest(hypothesis) == true) {
      result = observer.test(observer.currentKnowledge, hypothesis);
      // result is "refuted" or "notRefuted" (Popper)
   }
   else {
      result = "notTestable"; // neither "refuted" nor "notRefuted".
   }
   return result;
}
observer.canTest(hypothesis) returns true if the observer has provided a test for the hypothesis. Otherwise it returns false. In physics, this test must always be empirical. In other domains it could simply be a proof that the hypothesis contains unresolved problems or contradictions.
Hypotheses about the physical world that include infinite values are intrinsically untestable since no finite, empirical experiments can be created to test them. This means that any infinite value or singularity, such as the one that currently appears at the beginning of the Big Bang, must be ignored. It has the same status as "There are angels".
Changing the scientific method in this way means that all hypotheses, not just those relating to what we currently call science, become subject to the same methodology.
A hypothesis such as "There are angels" is "notTestable" until the test can be formulated. After that, the hypothesis can be either "refuted" or "notRefuted" .

The limits of clock time

First, a digression about causality:
Causality can only be infered retrospectively.
In order for a causal link to be established between two events, they both have to be available for inspection. Induction is possible for predicting future events, but its unreliable.
Example: Using memory of whole eggs, its possible for an observer to infer that a broken egg was once whole, and that some event caused it to break.
But it is impossible to know whether a whole egg will be still be whole some time in the future, when the observer looks at it again. Some event may or may not have caused it to break, or it might have disappeared altogether...
This means that it is a mistake to think that the Universe's evolution is exactly forseeable. On the contrary, its evolution is intrinsically unpredictable.
Physicists have constructed a causal chain backwards from the current now, and have calculated the age of the Universe to be ca. 13.8 billion years. There is no reason to doubt this figure. It is definitely finite.
But note that the causal link to the previous moment in the chain becomes "notTestable" when quantum effects and/or the distortion of spacetime make testability impossible.
So physical (clock) time is finite, and its beginning depends on where/when a test can be made as spacetime dissolves in quantum uncertainty.

Reconciling relativity and quantum mechanics

We can never, even in principle, observe events or objects:
  • outside the observable Universe. (On the very large scale, beyond the microwave background radiation.)
    Interestingly, we are at the centre of a sphere that is expanding at the speed of light, on which we can see a static picture of the microwave background radiation. We are told that the Big Bang didn't happen at any special place, but we are at the centre of our own observable universe.
    Ptolemy was right after all! Its just a question of perspective. :-)
  • Inside a Black Hole's event horizon. As with the microwave background radiation, objects falling into a Black Hole appear to us to stop on its event horizon.
  • Inside a Planck volume.
    I'm not a quantum physicist, so I asked Claude (an AI) that said:
    In most quantum gravity approaches, both space and time disappear from the fundamental equations, and must somehow emerge together at larger scales as approximate, classical concepts. How the familiar flowing time and extended space of everyday experience arise from a timeless, spaceless — or foamy, discrete — quantum substrate remains one of the greatest open questions in theoretical physics.
    According to my essay The Arrow of Time, perceived space and perceived time are created by consciousness, while spacetime is a mathematical model that is used to explain empirical facts beyond the range covered by the human senses.
    That space and time disappear at the quantum level in the spacetime model is simply saying that space and time become unobservable at that level — even when assisted by arbitrarily powerful apparatus.
So these regions:
  1. mark the limits of human perception (assisted by arbirarily powerful apparatus) and therefore the domain of experimental physics.
  2. are indistinguishable from one another.
    Relativity tells us what happens at the edge of the observable universe and on a Black Hole's event horizon. Quantum theory tells us what happens down to the Plank scale.
    The mathematics of relativity and quantum theory get the same result ("notTestable") when using the above corrected scientific method near the extreme limits of our perception. They also blend into each other, imperceptibly, at the level of our perceptions.
    So relativity and quantum mechanics can be simultaneously valid over the whole scale of possible observations. At the scale of human perception without extra apparatus they are both so weak that Newtonian physics can be used as a very good approximation.
    Its no surprise that we see different rules applying when we look down in scale, and when we look up. These are the rules we have discovered...

The limits of observation

As an observer, the time I have in which to observe the physical world
  • is obviously finite.
    This is the time in which my physical senses are working — roughly speaking, the time between my birth and death.
  • has no beginning.
    Following Heraclitus: I am not the same person as I was a moment ago because I now have new memories. And I am obviously not a new born baby, a fertilised egg, or even a twinkle in my father's eye etc.
    Observation is thus the process of gathering memories during now.
    The beginning of that now depends on how the memories are being formed — which is itself a process that has no well defined beginning.
This covers all the observations that I can make, not just to those related to the purely physical, outside world.
It also applies to my observations of
  • colours (qualia)
  • the memories stored in my physically observable brain.
  • any memories that might be stored beyond the dashboard of my experience. See Analytic Idealism.
Since the above list of things that I am not can be continued back to the Big Bang, and I am part of the Universe observing itself,  I think the process by which I am gathering memories must be part of the process that began with the beginning of time.

Footnotes

Analytic Idealism is the philosophy described in Bernardo Kastrup's Analytic Idealism Course.
I agree completely with Kastrup that there is an Interface of Perception, and that the scientific method should also apply to philosophy.
Kastrup calls the Interface of Perception a "dashboard", Donald Hoffman calls it a "desktop". I have chosen to use Kastrup's term.
I am, however, wary of using of words (like "mind") to describe the domain beyond the dashboard. I think it can only be investigated using the revised scientific method described in this essay. Progress has to be made using well defined empirical or logical tests — even in philosophy.
An observer is an ordinary human being
having:
  1. physically instantiated sense organs that can be used in conjunction with any physically instantiated apparatus. (thermometers, clocks, microscopes, telescopes, particle colliders etc.)
  2. internal processes such as thinking, introspection etc.
  3. a memory for recording past events.
    The memory could be stored entirely in the observer's physical brain, but some memories could also be stored "beyond the dashboard" of perception — the computer analogy would be "in the cloud".
Each observer is part of the universe being observed
The observer therefore necessarily has blind-spots.
Perceived space and time are part of the brain's strategy for reducing complexity.
(Bernardo Kastrup points out, in a memorable phrase, that brains would dissolve into an entropic soup if they tried to make a complete representation of the external Universe.)
Observations can only be made when the observer is conscious:
Consciousness is defined in my essay The Arrow of Time to be the awareness of space and time (as separate entities). Note, however, that space does not exist without perceived objects, and time does not exist without perceived events. So consciousness can also be defined as the awareness of objects and events.
Observations are made in the observer's here and now:
Observations cannot be made in the past or future, but they can be recorded or committed to memory so that the recordings or memory can be observed in a now at a later date.
Perceived time and Clock time are distinct types
Perceived (=observed) time flows in one direction, while clock time (used by physicists) is reversible. See The Arrow of Time.
Physicist's spacetime is a mathematical model of observed phenomena whose components are intrinsically reversible. I think this is because the measuring rods and clocks used to develop it are internally symmetrical: It makes no difference to Einstein's original argument if his measuring rods or clocks are reversed. In the case of clock time: its impossible to tell whether a film of a swinging pendulum is running forwards or backwards.
Infinite quantities cannot be observed
The observer's (finite) sensorium would need to be able to register infinitely large quantities. That is clearly impossible.
Ideally, models of the observable universe should use finitist mathematics but currently they don't, so infinities can appear in the calculations.
