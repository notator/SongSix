Philosophy (Free Will etc.)

No need to apologize for going off on a tangent!
Actually, I don't think you did (much). Your post was very stimulating.
You said:
For me determinism and Free Will are absolutely independent.
“Could have done otherwise”, is either completely misunderstood or irrelevant.
Agreed.
As explained in my text, I've come to the conclusion that the Universe is intrinsically evolving in an indeterministic way. This agrees with
  1. Tse's (bottom-up) argument from neurons
  2. The (top-down) observation that both physical and Darwinian evolution are intrinsically indeterminate. Planets crashing into each other, meteors destroying dinosaurs etc.
This attitude to the Universe means, I think, that:
  1. my Free Will is Libertarian.
  2. the Big Bang singularity problem can safely be ignored. The Big Bang is a point in time that's not part of the evolving Universe. Our mathematical models of the world beyond the dashboard are there to be useful. We will never find a model that is absolutely correct.
"My meta-reasoning examines my reasons and ultimately my action, therefore I have Free Will."
I'd strengthen your argument as follows:
Meta-reasoning explains your actions because it examines your reasons. So your actions depend on your reasons. But reasons don't always provide a specific answer, therefore you must have Free Will to overcome the uncertainty.
Free Will deniers ignore the fact that we often have to act when we have insufficient information.
It makes no sense to think that a superior intelligence, behind the dashboard, might be deceiving us into perceiving chaos when the reality is actually deterministic. That super-intelligence would be using an abstract kind of time, different from the time we actually experience, so our notion of causality would no longer apply. Evil demons cannot cause anything. :-)
I concluded the importance of Free Will must have developed in Anglican religion.
Nathan could tell us more about the history of Free Will, but I think its probably the answer to a very old problem — that became theological:
Human fathers know that their children have a Will of their own, and need some justification for correcting (even chastising) them when they do things that hurt themselves or others. In other words, why should a father have to give his children pain and punishment? The answer is that the children have Free Will, so are not under his control!
If God is made in the image of Man, we have to ask God the same question. But if, like most people nowadays, we don't do that, Free Will ceases to be an issue. Its just an observed fact.
Children, who don't yet know much about their environment, obviously have Free Will, so we do too! We don't know much more than the children do.
This story contains, I think, the answer to your search for a solution to how we learn to be good citizens in society. It also allows for the changing attitudes to how children should be taught (with less violence). I don't think we need to appeal to faith or metaphysics for that.

More biographical background

I was born and educated in England, but moved to Cologne, Germany in 1977, at the age of 29, partly for practical reasons related to my job. My philosophical roots are in the English empiricist tradition as it was in the 1950s and 60s (I was reading Bertrand Russell in my teens), but have long been confronted by the Arts world in Germany (which is still decidedly Romantic). Romanticism is anti-materialism. Its a dualistic answer to physicalism. Think Wagner/Nietsche.
I was baptised by an Anglican vicar at the age of 13, because my 23-year-old sister wanted to get married in church. My father, originally a Roman Catholic, was a professed atheist, and hadn't had any of his children baptised. It was great! All my previous sins were forgiven! :-) I have never been confirmed.
Scripture (Bible studies) was a standard subject at school until I was 14.
Basic knowledge of the Bible was thought to be culturally important.
Philosophically, I'm self-taught, which means that I probably have quite a few blind-spots. Discovered, and much appreciated, Bradley and Collingwood after finding Nathan's YouTube channel about a year ago. I was pretty shocked to discover that Bradley is only mentioned in passing in Russell's encylopedic History of Western Philosophy. Collingwood is not mentioned at all!
I moved to Germany not only because I had found the ideal job for my long-term project, but also because the Arts are taken more seriously on the Continent than in the UK. That's also a consequence of the German mistrust of pure materialism/physicalism which, I assume, has its roots in Kant.
So, English is my native language but the language I now speak every day is German. My written German is, however, still a bit shaky! I sympathise very much with those who have/need to write English, even though its not their native language! :-)
As a result of my job, working as a music copyist with an avant-garde composer and practising musicians, and the discipline imposed by programming, I think I've made some progress in the philosophy of time.
I now want to use my conclusions to attack the problem of "The Two Cultures", identified by C.P.Snow in 1959.
