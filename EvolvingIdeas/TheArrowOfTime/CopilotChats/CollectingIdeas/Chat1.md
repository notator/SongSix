Main old email (TODO: sort these ideas):<br>
The following arguments seem so obvious that I'm surprised nobody else has been putting them forward. Maybe they have, and I haven't noticed. Maybe its just that I have a rather special background, and that I've already isolated the two types of time. I'm looking from a special angle...<br>
One of Wolfram's key ideas is that of  "computational irreducibility". Behind that, I think, is the idea that everything can be described using the idea of a function. This is his contribution to a Theory of Everything - for computing a single output value from one or more input values.<br>
In addition to inputs and output, a function can also have an internal, current state, that affects the computation.<br>
We can define the word tick to mean the time it takes to run such a function.
The Arrow of Time:<br>
Using these definitions, a physical clock (such as the ones used by Einstein when constructing his theory of Relativity) is a simple binary function that returns a value that is the opposite of its input. Such a function has no current state. If the input is "to the right of the pendulum" then the output will be "to the left of the pendulum". This function can be run backwards, so neither classical nor quantum physics has a time direction, and physical time is reversible. There's no way to tell if a film of a swinging pendulum is running forwards or backwards. The ticks are of equal duration because the physical structure of the pendulum doesn't change.[**]<br>

---

The object that corresponds to a pendulum in the brain is a neuron. We don't have litte pendulums in our heads!<br>
A neuron's output is the signal it sends when its inputs satisfy some condition, so a neuron is the physical embodiment of a function that computes its single output using the values of several inputs. Such a function is irreversible. Its not possible to compute the values of the individual inputs from the output. So perceived time is irreversible. Its as simple as that! :-)<br>
This means that Entropy is essentially an observed phenomenon. Its not something that can be explained in terms of physical (reversible) time. I've never liked arguments that depend on probabilities. They always seem to be papering over the cracks...<br>
As a programmer, its quite interesting/useful to think about the input and output types used in these computations:<br>
For a physical clock, the function's input and output are both simple booleans (like true and false).<br>
For a neuron, the base type for the function's input values is "number", and the base type of its output value is "event".<br>
Note that a "number" is timeless, but that an "event" has something to do with time. The neuron function creates time. It makes a temporal measurement that generates a tick. A tick is the time since the function last ran.<br>
How the necessary sub-types are actually defined will depend on how we model the universe, but they don't affect the thrust of this argument. Currently I could imagine a "number" being either real, rational, irrational etc., and an "event" having a numeric "strength" parameter. But its quite possible for the sub-types to be types that have not yet been discovered. There's no guarantee that they can be discovered at all. -- which is an idea that relates to Analytic Idealism: Physical observations are always on the dashboard of experience.<br>
[**] This line of thinking also extends to space (The physical structure of Einstein's measuring rods doesn't change): In theoretical physics, there's no preferred direction. No absolute up or down, left or right. Even the number of dimensions is debatable. At the quantum level, the notion of locality itself seems to break down.
Its the act of measurement that brings both time and space into the picture, both for theoretical physicists and for the rest of us. Physicists have nothing to think about if they can't make any measurements. They therefore ignore the act, and just deal with the measurements.<br>
Remarks on the act of measurement:<br>
1. The act is necessarily registered by neurons, so perceived space and time are more fundamental than physical space-time.
2. When the act happens is irrelevant in physics. Physical experiments are supposed to be repeatable.

**Free Will**<br>
Wolfram has shown that en masse even the simplest cellular automata can behave unpredictably over time ("computational irreducibility"), so we can expect masses of neurons to be similarly unpredictable. I don't think there's any way for a brain (or any external entity) to actually understand the fundamental reasons for its decisions -- but I still think people should normally be treated as if they are responsible. I _want_ to be treated as if I'm responsible! That's also part of my brain state! :-)<br>
Our specific actions are both the result of a complex brain state and a cause of a new brain state (They change the context). But we have no way to access the details of our brain state except to say that we _feel_, or are inclined to act, in a particular way. The vast number of possible brain states means that we are each individually unique. Each of us has a unique history.<br>
Can _qualia_ can be approached by thinking about _feelings_?<br>

Synchronicity<br>
In physics, an event's position in space-time is described using Relativity. Objects can only be "synchronous" if they are at the same space-time.<br>
In brains, we perceive ourselves to be at a particular place in space and time. Events are said to be "synchronous" if we can't perceive any time difference between them. But, while we think of time as being continuous, we know that there is a lower threshold (around 1 millisecond) to the time we can actually perceive between any two events. We are only conscious of brain-state changes, and nothing in between.<br>
There are about 86 billion neurons in a human brain. They tick in parallel and irreversibly, and none of them ticks regularly when compared to a physical pendulum (metronome). In classical physical terms, (and contrary to Wolfram's cellular automata), neurons in a brain are obviously not ticking synchronously.<br>
Perceived time is defined by high level brain state changes (ticks), not by ticking neurons. But, since brains consist of neurons, their time is irreversible. They have no access to reversible time.<br>
Summary: the brain can be modelled as a function that has<br>
1. inputs, coming from the senses
2. a current state
3. output consisting of (parallel?) events on a single, irreversible time-line.
Note what I said in the previous email: Our perception of time "depends on what the brain's state was to begin with (including such parameters as "degree of concentration" etc.".<br>
I've long thought that "Space and time are the brain's strategy for reducing complexity". I think these arguments bring that idea to a new level. :-)<br>

I think there are three interesting (illuminating) examples (not given explicitly by Wolfram in the videos I've seen):<br>
    2a) when one of Einstein's clocks (used when he was developing relativity) ticks. Such ticks happen regularly because the time between them is related to the unchanging physical structure of the physical thing that is ticking (e.g. a pendulum or caesium crystal). This is what I call "physicalTime" in [2].<br>
    2b) when one of Wolfram's cellular automata cells changes state to generate the next generation of cells. In his cellular automata, all the cells in the same generation fire at once (are synchronised).<br>
    2c) when a neuron fires in the brain (reacting to changes in its inputs). These firings are fundamental state changes, but they are neither regular nor physically synchronised ! "perceivedTime" in [2] is a change of state of the whole brain. This is a highly complex state change that depends on what the brain's state was to begin with (including such parameters as "degree of concentration" etc.)<br>

Neurons

The basic argument is that he arrow of time is there because neurons can't be run backwards.<br>
Describing how neurons work involves the use/mention distinction. I'm using my own neurons to describe (i.e. mention) neurons. Its a strange but benign loop. Note that this agrees with Wolfram: computation is fundamental.<br><br>

Its interesting that neurons seem to us to have evolved. So the universe must have (or have evolved) an intrinsic arrow of time that allows it (us, as part of the universe) to observe itself. Neurons are the physical embodiment of a function that takes an input, and uses/defines time to create an output. The actual idea of a neuron is outside time.<br>

Note that neurons use energy to maintain their rest state. (The rest state is a static imbalance of electrochemical potential that is waiting to be released as a temporal impulse.) So the universe is essentially using neurons to create time out of energy! :-) At least that's the way it looks to us! :-) Neurons are actually made of physical stuff that can be described using (reversible time) physics. But their overall physical structure and function have to be described at a higher level.<br>

Consciousness

My position is that space and time are a brain strategy for reducing complexity. We are part of the Universe looking at itself, so we have something like a blind spot. An eye can't look at itself directly. The totality is more complex than can be observed by parts of it (individuals). The Universe (including us) can't be described completely using spatio-temporal dimensions, but we can describe large parts of it (when conscious).<br>
In other words: there is more to the Universe than human consciousness. Even the totality of individuals is not powerful enough to describe (or invent) the whole. I still have a sense of wonder...<br>
Apropos "Time is a prerequisite for consciousness": Note that we do not experience time (or space) when we are unconscious -- e.g. in deep sleep. Indeed, we don't experience anything when unconscious. Physical observations (e.g. that the Earth and Sun revolve about each other) are subject to the existence of (consciously) perceived time and space.<br>
So I think you are confusing subjective time with physical (clock) time if you think you could live longer (measured in Earth-years) just by measuring it in Mars-years.<br>
The subjective experience of time changes with the nature of our consciousness: In states of deep concentration (e.g. when writing an essay about time), we lose track of clock-time [1]. I think that's more important for subjective temporal experience than where (in space) we happen to be (on Earth or Mars)!<br>
Its simply a mistake to try to measure (more fundamental) subjective time in terms of (less fundamental) physical units. Consciousness is only of the subjective present. It has no access to the (physical) past or future. But the subjective present isn't just a physical instant (in space and time), it has a richness that goes beyond that. Wonder again...<br>

Conclusions<br>

Consciousness requires the experience of time and space. These are created by the brain using neurons. As I've said before: "Time and space are the brain's strategy for reducing complexity". But consciousness is more than that (qualia etc.)... Neurons are objects that have _evolved_ to create the experience of time and space out of energy. All is flux, so perceivedTime has no beginning, and since physicalTime is dependent on perceivedTime, it makes no sense to talk about the beginning of (reversible) pysicalTime. The brain creates a (probably imperfect) model of the world that allows it to observe itself. All is flux, so this is more than a survival strategy for individual species. I don't think the universe cares much about human survival. It didn't care much about the dinosaurs. :-)<br>

Guido d'Arezzo, Franco of Cologne:<br>
I see the development of early music notation as the gradual 'chunking' of temporal events so that the chunks can be fixed in space. Originally, sung chants were memorised without conscious chunking, so melismas were not differentiated from the "main" pitches. But chunking improves memorability, so it inevitably influenced the development of notation. A similar process also happened with ordinary text. A chunked concept can be associated with a written (spatial) symbol (such as "book"). Early neumes are only vaguely chunked... What they represent has rather vague borders, but proper noteheads represent well-defined, conceptual "events" (that can then be ornamented using ancillary symbols). Event symbols allow written music to be composed in advance (assuming everyone knows, subjectively, what the symbols are supposed to mean in practice).<br>
Discussions of tempo should always differentiate clearly between subjective and physical time. "Strict time" (Victor Silvester [2]) is a misunderstanding that equates (highly complex, will-driven) human bodies with simple mechanical devices. Dance rhythms involve acts of human will and effort to stay synchronous with the music, however (physically) "irregular" or "regular" the music may appear to be. In the case of a Viennese Waltz, it requires less effort to keep in time if the beat lengths relate to the physical necessities of the dance rather than to a physical metronome. Effort and human bodies are physical. Intention involves subjective time. Its the tension that makes dancing so entrancing. :-)<br>
