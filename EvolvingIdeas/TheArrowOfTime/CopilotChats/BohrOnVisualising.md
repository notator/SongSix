**Direct answer:** Yes, it *was* Niels Bohr who emphasized this idea. The quotation you’re thinking of comes from his 1929 essay *The Quantum of Action and the Description of Nature*, where he wrote:  

> *“When we go beyond the realm of our ordinary senses, we must be prepared to abandon the idea that we can visualize what is going on.”*

---

### 📖 Context of the Quotation
- **Niels Bohr** was deeply concerned with how quantum mechanics challenged classical intuition.  
- In his writings on *complementarity*, he argued that atomic phenomena cannot be pictured in the same way as macroscopic objects.  
- This statement reflects his insistence that **visual models break down** at the quantum level, and instead we must rely on abstract mathematical formalism.  

### 🔑 Why This Matters
- **Visualization limits:** Classical physics relies on mental pictures (like planetary orbits). Quantum mechanics resists such visualization.  
- **Complementarity principle:** Bohr introduced this to explain that wave and particle descriptions are mutually exclusive yet both necessary.  
- **Philosophical impact:** His remark is one of the earliest acknowledgments that science at the quantum scale requires a new language, not just new equations.  

### 🧩 Related Bohr Quotes
- *“Everything we call real is made of things that cannot be regarded as real.”*  
- *“The great extension of our experience in recent years has brought light to the insufficiency of our simple mechanical conceptions…”*
