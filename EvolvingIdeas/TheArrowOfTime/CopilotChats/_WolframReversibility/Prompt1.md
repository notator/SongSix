**Prompt** Can Stephen Wolfram's cellular automata be run backwards?<br>
**Short answer:** Some of Stephen Wolfram’s cellular automata can be run backwards, but not all. Only *reversible cellular automata* allow unique backward evolution, meaning every state has a unique predecessor. Most of Wolfram’s famous simple rules (like Rule 30 or Rule 110) are *not* reversible, so you cannot generally reconstruct the past from the present.  

---

### 🔄 Reversibility in Cellular Automata
- **[Reversible cellular automaton](guide://action?prefill=Tell%20me%20more%20about%3A%20Reversible%20cellular%20automaton)**: A system where every configuration has a unique predecessor. This means you can evolve the automaton both forward and backward in time.  
- **[Non-reversible rules](guide://action?prefill=Tell%20me%20more%20about%3A%20Non-reversible%20rules)**: Many of Wolfram’s well-known rules (e.g., Rule 30, Rule 110) are not reversible. Multiple different past states can lead to the same present state, so backward evolution is ambiguous.  
- **[Constructed reversible rules](guide://action?prefill=Tell%20me%20more%20about%3A%20Constructed%20reversible%20rules)**: Wolfram and others have designed special rules that are explicitly reversible, often by pairing states or using larger neighborhoods.

### 🌌 Why This Matters
- **Physics analogy**: Reversible CA resemble physical laws that conserve information, like classical mechanics.  
- **Irreversibility**: Non-reversible CA mimic thermodynamic processes where information about the past is lost.  
- **Wolfram’s vision**: In *A New Kind of Science*, Wolfram explores reversibility as a way to model fundamental physics, suggesting that the universe itself might be a reversible cellular automaton.  

---

### 🚀 Key Takeaway
Stephen Wolfram’s cellular automata *can* be run backwards **only if the rule is reversible**. Most of the simple, famous rules are not reversible, but specially constructed reversible automata exist and can evolve backward just as easily as forward.
