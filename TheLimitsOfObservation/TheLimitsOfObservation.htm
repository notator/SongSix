The general limits of observation

The time I have in which to observe the physical world
  • is obviously finite.
    This is the time in which my physical senses are working — roughly speaking, the time between my birth and death.
  • has no beginning.
    Following Heraclitus: I am not the same person as I was a moment ago because I now have new memories. And I am obviously not a new born baby, a fertilised egg, or even a twinkle in my father's eye.
    I must therefore be a process that is gathering memories in now.
    The beginning of that now depends on how memories are formed — which itself a continuous process (that is probably multi-threaded).
This applies to all the observations that I make while alive, not just to those related to the purely physical, outside world.
It also applies to observations of
  • the twinkle in my father's eye (external)
  • the memories stored in my physically observable brain (internal).
  • any memories that might be stored beyond the dashboard of my experience (internal). I'm an Analytic Idealist, so such memories can't be ruled out.

The limits of observation in physics

A remark on causality

Causality can only be determined retrospectively.
In order for a causal link to be established between two events, they both have to be available for inspection. Induction is possible for predicting future events, but it is unreliable.
Using memory of whole eggs, its possible for an observer to infer that a broken egg was once whole, but it is impossible to know whether a whole egg will be broken the next time the observer looks at it. The egg might even have disappeared altogether...
This means that it is a mistake to think that the Universe's evolution is forseeable. On the contrary, its evolution is intrinsically unpredictable.

The scientific method

In order to increase their knowledge, scientists use induction to create new hypotheses from existing knowledge. If a hypothesis survives an empirical test, they add it to their current knowledge. Otherwise they reject it and think again.
Unobservable hypotheses are illegal because they are not empirically testable, so the following pseudo-code includes a check for that, and returns omegaState (neither true nor false) if the check fails: 
    scientificMethod(observer, hypothesis)
   {
      if(observer.testable(hypothesis) == true) {
         result = observer.observe(observer.currentKnowledge, hypothesis);
         // result is true or false
      }
      else {
         result = omegaState; // neither true nor false.
      }
      return result;
   }
Using this correction, a singularity such as the one that currently appears at the beginning of the Big Bang, would simply not arise. Its place is taken by an omegaState. The singularity is just a programming error.

Illegal hypotheses

We can never, even in principle, observe events or objects:
  • outside our observable Universe. (On the very large scale, beyond the microwave background radiation.)
    Interestingly, we are at the centre of a sphere that is expanding at the speed of light, on which we can see a static picture of the microwave background radiation. Physicists tell us that the Big Bang didn't happen at any particular place (or time), but we are at the centre of our observable universe. Ptolemy was right after all! :-)
  • Inside a Black Hole's event horizon. As with the microwave background radiation, objects falling into a Black Hole appear to us to stop on its event horizon.
  • Inside a Planck volume.
    According to Claude (AI):
    In most quantum gravity approaches, both space and time disappear from the fundamental equations, and must somehow emerge together at larger scales as approximate, classical concepts. How the familiar flowing time and extended space of everyday experience arise from a timeless, spaceless — or foamy, discrete — quantum substrate remains one of the greatest open questions in theoretical physics.
    According to my essay The Arrow of Time, perceived space and perceived time are created by consciousness, while spacetime is a mathematical model that is used to explain empirical facts beyond the range covered by the human senses.
    That space and time disappear at the quantum level in the spacetime model is simply equivalent to saying that space and time become unobservable at that level — even when assisted by arbitrarily powerful apparatus.
So these regions:
  1. mark both the spatial limits of human perception (assisted by arbirarily powerful apparatus) and the domain of experimental physics.
  2. are indistinguishable from one another.
    Relativity tells us what happens at the edge of the observable universe and on a black hole's event horizon. Quantum theory tells us what happens down to the Plank scale.
    The mathematics of relativity and quantum theory therefore give the same result (omegaState) beyond the extremities of our perception, and imperceptibly blend into each other at the level of human perception.
    It looks as if relativity and quantum mechanics are simply working in parallel everywhere, but that we only see their effects at the appropriate scales.
    Its no surprise that we see different rules applying when we look down in scale, and when we look up. These are the rules we have discovered...

Conclusion

That perceived time has no beginning in the Heraclitian model contrasts strangely with the omegaState in the above account of physical observability.
The omegaState must therefore be one of the blind-spots that result from our being observers trying to observe the universe of which we are part.
