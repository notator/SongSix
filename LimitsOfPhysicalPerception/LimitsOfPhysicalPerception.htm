The Limits of Physical Perception

This problem is often explained using an analogy with spherical topology: The surface of a sphere is of finite size, but it has no origin. However, I think that analogy is misleading: It leads to absurd ideas like "circular time".
I am an observer. All my observations are made in my present here and now.
The past is inferred using incremental backward deduction. If a deduction can't be made, because it would result in an absurdity (non-existent infinities etc.), it simply stops. Therefore there is no deduceable beginning at "the beginning of time" and hence no singularity. Such cancellations are not unusual in mathematics or programming. They are just boundary conditions in the model.
To see why I don't begin at a particular moment, consider the following argument (Its practically Heraclitus'.):
Since my memories are different from those I had yesterday, I am not the same person I was yesterday, nor am I the person I was at school. I am not a new born baby or a fertilised egg cell either. I'm not even a twinkle in my father's eye or a twinkle in his father's eye etc. So I have no beginning in time. "Am" is not "was". I am only a process happening now.
This argument can also be made spatially: My body is not just the sum of its parts, and each part is not the sum of its parts, and so on down to the Planck volume.
So I think of myself as continuously evolving. Every new experience changes who I am. We are all part of the Universe looking at itself, so that's probably the best way to think about the Universe too.
Note carefully, that this argument uses perceived time, not the clock time used by mathematical physicsts. As I've shown elsewhere, clock time depends on perceived time, so we can also make a version for physicists that uses clock time.
The conclusion that time and space are finite but have no limits provides a possible answer to the theoretical conflict between Relativity and Quantum Physics.
Time ceases to contain observable events, and space ceases to contain observable objects in each of the following borderline cases:
We can never, even in principle, observe:
  • Events or objects outside the observable Universe. (On the very large scale, beyond the microwave background radiation.)
    Interestingly, we are at the centre of a sphere that is expanding at the speed of light, on which there is a static picture of the microwave background radiation. Both time and space stop there. Physicists tell us that the Big Bang didn't happen at any particular place (or time), but for an observer on Earth the situation is strangely ptolemaic! :-) We are at the centre of the observable universe. From the Roman Catholic Church's point of view, Ptolemy was right after all! Its just a question of perspective! :-)
  • Events or objects inside a Black Hole. As with the microwave background radiation, objects falling into a Black Hole appear to us to stop on its event horizon.
  • Anything below the Planck scale.
