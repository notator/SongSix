Preamble

This is a fascinating interview for anyone interested in the philosophy of mind, but watching it live wasn't nearly as productive as watching it again and being able to pause, rewind and take notes. Even if you missed the live event, don't miss the recording! :-)
The two questions that I asked live had been going through my mind before the interview started. Its very difficult for short questions to be really understood without knowing their context, so I wasn't really surprised when Prof. Tse's answers didn't really tell me what I wanted to know. Ordinary language needs context if its going to be understood... Even live keyboard chatting wouldn't have helped -- its impossible to type while following what's being said.
Public conversations between experts are changing the way philosophy can be done, but I think its the recordings that are important, not the ability to ask live questions.
The experts are talking informally, ignoring institutional boundaries, and are not being interrupted by newbie questions, so they stay on topic and can investigate areas they aren't sure about. That makes things especially interesting for outsiders. The form is like written media, in which one can pause and re-read, but less formal.

Notes on the interview itself

Here's a digest of my written notes, taken while viewing the recording.
Thoughts on my live questions follow below.
I use Kastrup's term "dashboard" to mean the interface between observables (objects and events) and the world as it really is. This is also the "desktop" in Donald Hoffman's Interface Theory of Perception.
2:30 Nathan: "As an idealist I don't feel the need to explain things primarily in terms of the physical."
I'm an idealist too, so I agree with that of course. (I'd probably have said "fundamentally" rather than "primarily".) But I also think that concrete observations (on this side of the dashboard) tell us something about the world as it really is (beyond the dashboard), so we need to take them seriously. I'm sure Nathan does too: that's why he's interviewing Prof. Tse.
BTW: I think the importance of "concrete observations" means that philosophy needs an interdisciplinary component (c.f. Collingwood). Of course, professional philosophy is also a discipline! :-)
3:55 Tse's definition of Libertarian Free Will. See also §2.5 and §2.6 in his course.
I act as if I have the kinds of Libertarian Free Will that Tse is describing, but believe that I am acting without complete information. That's something we all have to do in many situations. So, basically, I don't think I need to have a neurological reason to believe in Free Will, but am curious to see if Prof. Tse can convince me that there is one. The question is useful for Prof. Tse, because it drives his research (Collingwood again). No research would be possible if questions like that weren't asked. See also Question 1 below.
6:12 I agree very much with Tse that we are sculpting what happens out of what might potentially happen.
30:54 Tse explains why determinism at the level of the whole universe is unknowable.
This has to do with the fact that we are part of the Universe trying to observe itself. I use an identical argument in my (March 2026) essay The Arrow of Time .
33:27 Tse says that whether to believe this kind of determinism or not becomes a leap of faith.
An alternative (?) would be to believe that the problem lies beyond any brain's capacity to understand it. Preserve the wonder. :-)
33:45 Tse says that the arguments in favour of indeterminism are hugely superior.
Agreed. This is the best that can be done, given our limited capacity for understanding.
35:50 Tse: "The only indeterminism that really has been proven to exist, at least on the basis of data, is at the quantum level..."
Darwinian evolution may not be provable on the basis of data, but I think we all accept that its true. I think its also indeterministic.
To be clear, I don't think Free Will can depend on the fundamental properties of physical objects (strings and quarks) or their low-level interactions (quantum mechanics, non-locality etc.). What we think might potentially happen depends on our memories of what previously happened, not on the way those memories might be stored physically. That would be a category error.
Neurons use a logic that is very different from on/off logic gates (transistors). This difference makes the discussion of determinism versus indeterminism as a binary alternative rather suspect. I don't think we can assume that simple binary logic or "quantum indeterminacy" apply beyond the dashboard.
Neuronal logic is more fundamental than the logic used in physics. Without neurons, there would be no observation, and therefore no physics.
26th April 2026
I've now read §4.8 and §4.9 of Tse's online course, and now think I was wrong — but interestingly so!
The Universe is indeterministic (unpredictable) looking both from the top-down and bottom-up. So its okay for Tse to describe the indeterminism of neurons as amplification of physical effects (including quantum indeterminism). This has deeply to do with the nature of time (memory, evolution etc.).
38:36 Tse starts talking about patterns. See also §4.5 in his online video course.
Its very interesting that the "patterns" apply in both spatial and temporal domains. Objects "fit" into receptors of the right shape. Patterns of neuron firings "match" expected temporal modes. This means that "patterns" could correspond to something beyond the dashboard, where space and time are not separate. (I think consciousness is the awareness of space and time as separate entities.) Essentially, patterns are at a higher level of information than their components. The information they carry is therefore of a different class/type than the information carried by their components.
What I'd really like to know is how, physically, neurons recognise temporal patterns. How are brains affected by learning? Maybe the answer is to be found in the parts of Tse's course that I haven't yet read! (I'm about to start on §4.8)
45:39 "Neuroscience 101",
49:35 synaptic gaps
50:55 "Why have a gap at all?": "A neuron has to be able to specify its own conditions for firing."
Neurons are like functions in programming languages. They are blocks of separate but connected code. If they weren't separate, there would/could be no high-level structure.
Question: Could it be that the synaptic gap is tuned not for time, but for something else (e.g. minimum neurotransmitter/information loss while keeping the neurons independent)? The (mean) time taken for the gap to be crossed could be a by-product of the optimization. I take the point that the actual time associated with the signal (computed in some way from the wave of neurotransmitters) will not be exactly predictable from the signal, so indeterminacy may enter the picture here. And that can have consequences, especially if neurons are treating their input information analogically.
55:00 Nathan's summary
1:01:54 Interesting exchange about Second Order Free Will. Tse brings in the Nicomachean Ethics.

Question Time

1:05:53 Question 1: "What are the significant consequences for an individual of having or not having Free Will? Is this a question that's only sociologically relevant (for crime/punishment etc.)?"
What I couldn't explain in the question is that I think there are questions that can/should/could be unasked. I don't think it makes any difference to me personally whether my Will is actually Free or not. It feels free to me, so I act as if it is, even if it turns out in the end not to be. Whatever the answer turns out to be, I don't think it would change the way I act.
Tse's answer linked the problem to hope, and to the importance of cultivating good habits. The Nicomachean Ethics again. :-) But that's the answer to why I should act as if I have Free Will, not the question of whether I really do have it.
My actions in relation to other people are such as to optimize my experience with other people. If society thinks it will benefit from treating people as if they have Free Will (and passes laws accordingly), then of course that's what I will do. That answer to the problem is also pragmatic rather than scientific.
So, while I now think there may indeed be a relation between neurology and Free Will, I don't really need to know that there is such a relation. As I said above: The question is important for Prof. Tse because it drives his research. No research would be possible if questions like that weren't asked.
If there is an ultimate answer, it involves a sequence of events, and therefore the fundamental nature of time. As an Idealist, I think that is inscrutable (beyond the dashboard).
Prof. Tse is assuming sequential - one directional - time, as dictated by (his and our) neurons, rather than the reversible time used in physics.
How the brain (time) really works (beyond the dashboard) is not accessible to direct observation. All systems that try to observe themselves have blind spots...
1:21:41 Question 2: "Sodium-Potassium pumps store potential energy by effectively running negative entropy. How is it that nature automatically uses ATP to keep this process going? (I've got to the end of §3 in Prof. Tse's course.)"
(I'm still following the course with great interest. Have now completed §4.7).
Its a mystery to me that nature has found a way to construct all the complex proteins involved, and store potential energy by burning ATP to effectively reverse the usual direction of entropy.
Asserting that simple Darwinian evolution got us here seems pretty hand-wavy. I'd like the individual steps to be explained, even if the answer is rather long, before letting that pass.
At the moment, it looks to me as if nature has invented a perpetual motion machine simply to create neurons that fire/tick according to complicated inputs (as opposed to ordinary clocks whose regular ticks are simply related to their unchanging structure)! As I've said elsewhere, I still can't rule out intelligent design by something that is beyond our comprehension.
