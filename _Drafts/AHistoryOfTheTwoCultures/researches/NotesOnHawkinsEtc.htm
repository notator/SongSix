R.G. COLLINGWOOD: Interview with Dr. Maarten Steenhagen

I agree  a lot with Collingwood! :-)
40:54 Critiquing Analytic Philosophy's methodology.
I think propositional logic is part of maths. Its symbols are timeless in the proofs. But there are no equations in ordinary language because the meanings of its symbols depend on their context. "Henry" at the start of a novel is someone else at the end of the novel. Maths models the physical world (beyond the dashboard), not the perceived world. I agree with Wolfram that computation (as in the function defined by a neuron) is fundamental to perception. We dont have little crystals in our heads. I think the attempt to use maths in philosophy is the result of physics envy. The scientific attitude is correct -- we should be open minded, but reject theories that are demonstrably wrong. Popper: Conjectures and Refutations is the way to make progress, but mathematical physics doesnt apply at higher levels of information (proteins etc.) We've made a lot of progress since WW2. Programming languages are a new kind of writing, that embody clarifications of many crucial concepts: functions, interfaces etc. Wooly concepts like "emergence" have been sorted out.
1:05:28 Philosophical realism's effect on our conception of truth
Hawkins tells a nice story of someone who was rejected as an Anglican Vicar because he believed the story of Genesis. Hawkins (elsewhere) gives an account of the genesis story as if its describing the Genesis of becoming human from birth: babies first have no idea what or where they are (i.e. chaos). They then go through varous stages, ending with the knowledge of good and evil. I've never thought of Genesis in that way, but its enlightening!
1:05:40 Apropos "humans were made in the image of God". My "axiom" or "deep insight" (that we are part of the universe looking at itself) resonates closely with that! :-)
1:06:20 - ca. 1:09:08 Hawkins finds it strange that the church believes that the scientific (biological) narrative is true, and then sings songs but doesnt take them seriously... I think this whole discussion would benefit from distinguishing between how math (science) symbols mean (beyond time) and language (art) symbols mean (context dependence).
1:07:54 Steenhagen says "the truth claims that you get in both these fields..." He's talking about the real world and novels... I think he's saying that songs in the real world are on a par with the world of fiction, but that's to ignore the possibility of transcendence in the immediate experience in the song. Collingwood's realms of truth need looking at a little more closely!
1:09:08 Hawkins talks about the relativity of beliefs in different cultures. I agree with him, but think the pressing project is to get our own house in order. Western culture is currently in crisis because the Arts and Sciences have become seriously disconnected, and because the academic institutions in which they are cultivated are in crisis. That's our problem.
1:11:35 Steenhagen: "How do you think you can have knowlegde of beauty if you have never tried to realize beauty in the world? Its insane!" Exactly. Children should be taught respect for artistic creation by being asked to create things themselves. Even if the student isn't good at it, he/she should learn to respect the attempt and effort. In my experience, Artists are respected much more in Germany than in England. This is obviously related to the different philosophical traditions (pragmatism/idealism).
1:37:19* Kinds of philosophy and where they might lead:
For me its about sorting out hierarchies (something I've got from following the development of programming languages over the past 50 years). Expert knowledge should be respected at all levels: Historians of philosophy know best about their subject. Bakers know best how to bake good bread. Govenrnment should be done by those those who know best how to govern. Voting for descision makers should therefore be hierarchically organized.  In government, the top executive should be elected by his peers, not by the general public. Its right that the Prime Minister of the UK or the Chancellor in Germany are elected by their parliaments, but a mistake for the President of the USA to be elected by the general public who have no idea how the world works at that level.
1:44:30 etc. Steenhagen: "Collingwood would say you really need to do the hard work you need to do the digging and you need to look at [...] what are the foundations, where do we want to go, what are the what are the concepts that people Now understand. How can we use those Concepts to articulate a view of society of knowledge of philosophy that enables us to move towards a a better world a better society..." Is that a call for going back to first principles: treat hisory of philosophy as a background, but start with direct observation of the current, practical world? I think that's what I'm doing. But remember that I am the product of my milieu and education. Historical connections will happen automatically. without explicit references to what anyone has previously thought. Of course, it helps to have historical knowledge, a respect for logic etc. That's the culture of which I'm part.
1:44:34 SteenHagen "There's really no easy recipe to do that. You just need to sit down and write the book..." I think Marx, like me, would have thought that that's not enough. The masses can't be expected to read the book. If you want to change anything in the real world, you have to interact with people directly. Maybe its enough to talk to people who are going to talk to the right people. Books can, of course be influential -- if they become bestsellers among society's decision makers.
1:53:56* Hawkins: "How does logic fit into this picture?" I think the development of programing languages after WW2, grew out of the concern with formal logic in the earlier part of the century. This development has given us a new kind of writing, somewhere between maths and ordinary language, that enables us to communicate with machines. To write using these languages, one needs both rigorous discipline and creativity...

21:13 "if [clockTime and perceivedTime] simply came apart [...] that would be big news. And I think bad news for a lot of traditional philosophy of time." Yes indeed! :-)
21:37 The second Law of thermodynamics:
23:19 "there are models of the universe in which there are regions in which there's a thermodynamic gradient, but it points in the opposite direction to us..." We dont need that idea: Sodium potassium pumps store energy using the universal gradient...
35:50 etc. He starts talking about life, but I think he's still too close to material physics, in that he's still talking about the creation of hydrogen in stars. Life works at a higher level of  information than atoms. Proteins etc. Sodium Potassium pumps in neurons... I think he's missing the concept of two types of time.
40:00 etc. He acknowledges the psychological aspect of time, so I shouldn't get too uppity about that. But I still think he's conflating (or trying to conflate) physicist's time with psychological time. He just doesn't have the vocabulary to differentiate the two.
46:24 "There is no fundamental Arrow of Time". I think that's true for clockTime, but not for the Universe. I think the universe is evolving intrinsically, and that our memories record (or try to record) what happens to us given that space and time are brain strategies for reducing the universe's intrinsic complexity. We have no way of knowing what's really going on with the universe (our concepts and language are inadequate), but we do know that we perceive in terms of space and time.
ca 1:00:00 He seems to be in favour of bi-directional time wrt quantum theory (Bell's theorem). I agree with him there. If the maths predicts the results we experience, then so be it! But he's talking about physical time here (modelled by maths) beyond our dashboard, not perceived time. Its the confusion of the two types that gets people confused.

On Stockhausen

Stockhausen wrote ...how time passes... in 1962. This describes how he thought of time at the time. The article was very influential in the avant garde music community but was ignored by the philosophical community who were still under the spell of physics. Interesting that the avant garde music community were also trying to be "scientific" at the time.

Ongoing Conclusions

I'm afraid I'm wasting my time listening to Kripal too much, even though much of what he says may be true. However, listening to him has lead me think a bit more about how to handle my beliefs -- especially those that I regard as "soft" or "unreliable". These include all beliefs that depend on connections beneath the surface (beyond the dashboard):
  • Wigner's friend
  • Precognition/Telepathy
  • Psychosomatic effects
  • Near death experiences
  • UFOs
  • The "collective subconscious" -- non-physical, but inherited cultural phenomena
I dont think such beliefs can be converted into hard beliefs just by believing them. The only way to progress is (as with Wigner's friend) demonstrate the circumstances in which they have consequences. Otherwise my reaction remains "So what?".
In the English pragmatic tradition, I'm looking for ways to change the world (not just describe it). I'm really only interested in beliefs that have consequences. I agree, of course, with Einstein that it is essential to preserve a sense of wonder.
