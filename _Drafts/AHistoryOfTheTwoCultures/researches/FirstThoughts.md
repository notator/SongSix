#### A History of The two Cultures (was Chaos in the Arts and Sciences)

The Two Cultures problem arose, I think, because mathematical physics cannot, after all, provide a complete _Theory of Everything_. An alternative, non-mathematical culture therefore came into existence. To see how that may have played out, and to see how computers change the game, I'd now like to review the history of Chaos Theory.

This is a huge subject, about which whole books could be written. A proper treatment of the subject would be out of place here, but I need to say at least _something_ about it even if I get a few details wrong. (These can be corrected later.)

Towards the end of the 19th century, both Artists and Scientists were noticing (and having to cope with) the limits of the theories that had developed over the preceding 500 years. Cultural evolution is the inevitable result of human creativity.  
Very broadly, the following correspondences seem to have arisen:

| ca. | Arts | Sciences | Concept |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1860- | Impressionism | Thermodynamics,  <br>Biology (Darwin) | High-level descriptions |
| 1905- | Expressionism | Relativity | Observer-dependence |
| 1920- | Cubism | Quantum Mechanics | Non-locality |

The Arts and Sciences had diverged to become completely separate intellectual spheres (especially following the Romantic movement), so these parallels are separate, independent developments. They are the result of common, underlying currents, not a direct interrelation between the cultures.

In the Arts, Stravinsky’s _Sacre du Printemps_ (which could be classified as Expressionist) is quintessentially chaotic. His later, neo-classical, work plays with expectations in other ways (polytonality etc.) often associated with Cubism.  
In the Sciences, Thermodynamics is associated with Boltzmann, but _[Chaos Theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chaos_theory)_ is generally thought to have been initiated by Maxwell and Poincaré.

After World War Two, the situation escalates further. Currents overlap even more, but there are still discernible parallels:

| ca. | Arts | Sciences | Concept |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1946- | Abstract expressionism | Information Theory, cybernetics | Dynamic, evolving processes |
| 1960s | Minimalism | Systems theory & early self‑organization | Simple rules leading to complex emergent behavior |
| 1970s | Concept Art,  <br>New Complexity | Chaos Theory | Unpredictable outcomes |

Some important composers in this period are: Stockhausen, Boulez, Berio, Xenakis, Ligeti, Cage, Feldman, Reich, Riley, Ferneyhough.  

In the 1950s and 1960s, experimental notations were a hot topic because standard music notation had finally broken down completely. The experiments collapsed quite suddenly in 1970, without having solved anything, because composers couldn't sustain their careers while simultaneously trying to invent notations. The whole system was out of control. Chaos reigned supreme.  
Before 1970, live improvisation and composition directly on tape media had been an answer. Stockhausen and Boulez were using "precise" serial techniques, but the results were frustratingly similar to "imprecise" operations. Cage and Xenakis had embraced chance and probabilistic techniques.  
After 1970, having solved nothing, most of the above composers reverted to unashamed pragmatism. The _New Complexity_ movement (Ferneyhough and others) were special in that they treated notational chaos as being inevitable — and as having value in itself.

Important figures in computing and _Chaos Theory_ in this period include: Turing, Norbert Wiener, John von Neumann, Edward Lorenz.

1970-1974, while still a student, and later, I worked for the music publisher Universal Edition, actually copying many of their printed scores of Feldman, Earle Brown etc.  
I became Stockhausen's copyist 1974-2000 so that I could stay close to the problems. Copying scores and being in close contact with practising musicians meant that, unlike Wolfram, **I was always confronted by the difference between clock time and perceived time (music has to be performed ), while simultaneously grappling with the concepts of space (on the page) and time.**

1980s- Murray Gell‑Mann, Benoit Mandelbrot, Mitchell Feigenbaum, Stephen Wolfram, Stuart Kauffman (biology).  

As a composer, I was involved with algorithmic composition of spatial objects (scores). **So, for me, the diagrams produced by Wolfram's cellular automata looked like algorithmic** _**drawing**_**. They were scores that needed interpretation. Wolfram is making the same mistake that was made by composers in the 1950s - trying to equate space with perceived time.**
