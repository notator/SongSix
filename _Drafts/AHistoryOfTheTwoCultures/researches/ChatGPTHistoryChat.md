The **Mandelbrot set was first defined and drawn in 1978** by **Robert W. Brooks and Peter Matelski**, in the context of their work on Kleinian groups. Wikipedia

Benoit Mandelbrot’s role came slightly later: in **1980**, while working at IBM, he produced the first high‑quality computer visualizations of the set, which led to its widespread recognition and the association of his name with it. Wikipedia

### How the definition emerged

*   **1978 — Formal definition** Brooks and Matelski introduced the set while studying iterations of complex functions related to Kleinian groups. Their work included the first images, though very coarse by modern standards.
    
*   **1980 — Mandelbrot’s visualization breakthrough** Mandelbrot used IBM’s computational resources to generate detailed renderings. These images revealed the set’s extraordinary structure and self-similarity, sparking the modern study of fractal geometry.
    
*   **1982–1985 — Public impact** Mandelbrot’s book _The Fractal Geometry of Nature_ (1982) and the 1985 _Scientific American_ cover featuring the set helped cement its cultural and mathematical significance. MathWorks
    

### Why the date matters

The distinction between **definition** (1978) and **popularization** (1980 onward) is important because it shows how mathematical ideas often become famous only after computational visualization makes their structure visible.
