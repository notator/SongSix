This text is a reaction to the interview between Dr. Nathan Hawkins and Dr. Steven Meyer, published on 22nd May 2026.
Pushing Stephen Meyer further on his argument for God

Preamble

Briefly: I think Meyer is a dangerous demagogue, but that carefully investigating his shortcomings has been well worth while.
It has forced me to think about my own position with respect to religion, and has resulted in an interesting hypothesis that could lead to the reconciliation of relativity and quantum mechanics..
I agree very much with Nathan (and Collingwood) that the neglect of this subject by academic philosophers, and their retreat from the public discourse, has had a catastrophic effect on public culture.
But its obviously a minefield. The only answer is serious philosophy.
Remember Nathan's outline of his project, and Kastrup's praise for his courage in The Importance of Idealism to Culture and Life.

Reactions to a first viewing

I found watching this talk for the first time rather frustrating: Nathan didn't really push Meyer's arguments enough. Maybe he could clear up some of the  questions raised here in a second interview.
Philosophical debate should be respectful and polite, but it should also be rigorous. Philosophy can't be done properly if serious questions are never discussed or even allowed to surface.
My first thoughts:
I don't rule out the existence of a superior intelligence that "created the Universe", but had the feeling that Meyer was simultaneously over- and underestimating the God he was arguing for:
  1. Meyer's God surpasses human understanding in many ways, so what use is that God to us? We act according to our own (limited) understanding, so what does Meyer think are the consequences for us of believing that He exists?
    My own view is that the anthropomorphism involved in believing in such a God invalidates the idea. Its a form of hubris that ends up causing endless confusion.
    I have a deep sense of wonder at the beauty of the Universe but using words like "create" in this context does not help.
  2. The Explanatory Power argument cuts both ways: Does the God hypothesis explain too much? If it explains everything, then why bother with philosophy? We could just do theology instead. And what consequences would that have?
    If the hypothesis doesn't explain everything, then philosophers will be interested in what it doesn't explain. In other words: An omnipotent God is incompatible with philosophy. If the hypothesis doesn't explain everything, then it doesn't explain enough.
    Given a Creator, what are the remaining philosophical questions, according to Meyer?
  3. Is Meyer's God anything more than a materialist, Darwinian Creator -- who knows nothing about love? Is this just the Old Testament Creator? Why is the concept of love central to Christianity? A discussion of the Trinity might have been quite enlightening! How does Meyer's God relate to the world we observe, in all its complexity.
In the interview with Peter Tse, Nathan said (at 2:30)
"As an idealist I don't feel the need to explain things primarily in terms of the physical."
Meyer ought to be pressed more on high-level, non-material aspects of the world we experience.

Nathan apologised for not being able to cover Meyer's arguments properly in the given time so, before commenting on the interview, I started by watching the trailer to Meyer's new film. Here are some comments on that.

1:55 narrator: "So we have two great competing stories about reality. One posits a purposeful Creator behind the Universe. The other envisions mindless processes producing everything we see."
This is a false dichotomy. There are problems with both alternatives:
  • The Creator hypothesis is dealt with below.
  • The Mindless Processes story assumes that only material objects exist.
    Idealists deny that that is the case.
Meyer is a mind+matter dualist who doesn't understand idealism, so he ignores it. If he does understand idealism (as he says), then he is deliberately ignoring it, which is even worse.
Idealism makes no fundamental assumptions at all about what is beyond the dashboard of experience, except that it appears to "exist".
As I understand it, Analytic Idealism (as proposed by Kastrup) is an evolving philosophy, that relies on empirical data and the scientific method, developed over the past 350 years or so, to drive its progress.
I think of myself as being an Analytic Idealist, but my own current position (in contract to Kastrup's) is that using any words at all to describe the world beyond the dashboard is to overestimate the power of human language. If there were to be something like a "creator" or "mind" there, it would be inaccessible to human understanding. We just have to accept that we have blind-spots — and hold on to our sense of wonder. Linguistic speculation can be helpful if it is logical and free of contradictions, but ultimately the best tool for investigating the realm beyond the dashboard is mathematical modelling combined with empirical data (i.e. the scientific method).
4:52 Meyer: "Before the beginning of time, there is no Universe."
"The beginning of time" is part of Meyer's (our) mathematical model of the universe. Its the model that is having problems, not the Universe.
5:16 anon1: "We are being forced to accept, by the observations, that the Universe is evolving and it had a beginning."
This is just wrong. Nobody has ever observed the beginning of the Universe. And just because the Universe is temporally finite does not mean that it has a beginning. For an account of how that can be, and what consequences it has, see The Philosophical Method on my website.
Meyer might do better to shift his ground by appealing to his God's transcendence of time. That would allow the Creator to intervene (create) continuously over perceived time, but it would also raise different, possibly insoluble questions — such as: Why don't we perceive objects suddenly popping into existence? Well, maybe we do, but only when using special apparatus, at the quantum level!
5:30-7:20 Fred Hoyle and the problem of fine tuning.
If we continue to deduce ever earlier states of the Universe from its present state (Remember: all observations are made in and of the present state.) Its no wonder that we get results consistent with the current state of the universe. It would be very surprising if we didn't.
Hoyle and his contemporaries were surprised, because they thought of the universe as an objective entity, separate from themselves. They, and we, are however part of the universe we are observing, so we can only observe one universe — the one we are in! This is just a strong version of the anthropic principle (Which Meyer fails to mention.)
See also The Limits of Physical Perception.
7:57 McGrew: "Once of the most curious attempts to get around inference to design in the universe is to push it all off onto the concept of a multiverse."
As described in the previous comment, we can only ever observe the universe we are part of so, just as with intelligent designers, there is no point in speculating about multiverses. Both are redundant concepts.
8:30 Thiel: "The multiverse is like this gateway drug. Once you are lost in the multiverse, you might as well be in a simulation."
8:34 anon2: "The simulation theory? That belongs in the movies."
Agreed! :-) The irony here is that this trailer is using the techniques of advertising (failure to mention the competition, emotional music) to sell its ideas. Going by the trailer, the movie must be doing the same. These are warning flags. Films about popular science and philosophy that want to be taken seriously are generally much more thoughtful, and less emotional.
12:25 narrator: (Meyer?) "The fact that the Universe came into being at a definite moment seems to me theological. There is evidence for what can only be described as a supernatural event."
As described above, I don't think the Universe had a beginning — but I can't rule out the possibility that it was created by some kind of superintelligent being outside time, beyond the dashboard of our physical senses.
As I said above, the traditional Creator hypothesis (as set out in Genesis Chapter 1) has several serious problems:
  1. It leads to an infinite regression (How was the Creator created?)
  2. How and why did the Creator choose that particular moment?
  3. Why does evil exist if the Creator is omnipotent?
    (Later chapters in the Bible concede that evil does exist. Some chapters are concerned, for example, with the killing of innocent children: The story of Abraham's son, the Massacre of the Innocents...)
  4. Why, having been created, do living things die?
  5. Is death always an evil?
So Meyer, in jumping to Genesis Chapter 1 has inadvertently introduced the concept of evil. But evil is only perceived by humans (who have physical senses and a perception of space and time). There is no evil in the material world. So the Creator of the material world (if He exists) is blind to evil, and cannot be omnipotent.
The Abrahamic religions have different answers to this situation. As I understand it, Christians accept the existence of evil as a fact but believe in the Trinity: Father, Son and Holy Spirit. Christ provides the senses that his Father does not have. He can therefore perceive evil and provide the antidote (love).

Detailed (theological) comments (viewing for a second time)

In Nathan's defence, I should first acknowledge that doing a written analysis of Meyer's position, using the recording, is much easier than doing it in a live interview. Nathan did a pretty good job under the circumstances! But I think we need a more rigorous discussion if we want to make progress and arrive at some kind of metaphysical consensus.
4:34 Meyer: "... materialism itself is increasingly a spent force as an explicitly held philosophy."
Agreed, but Meyer continues to base his arguments on ideas coming from material physics and cosmology. Isn't that rather inconsistent? He professes to be a (mind+matter?) dualist. How does the collapse of materialism affect that?  Like Nathan, I don't think he has really understood what idealism is, or what it has to offer. 
9:02 The Kalam argument for God
This argument is not explained in the interview, but goes as follows:
  1. The Universe had a beginning;
  2. Whatever has a beginning has a cause;
  3. The Universe therefore has a cause, which must be a transcendent God.
9:20 Meyer: "My way of formulating the cosmological argument from the beginning is a bit more subtle than a straightforward Kalam argument."
9:38 Meyer: "I would in brief say that the evidence that we have of the beginning [of the Universe] — which I think is very strong but can't be proven with absolute certainty because nothing in science can be — but all the pointers are to a beginning... The best explanation of that putative fact is the existence of something like a theistic or a deistic god — a transcendent mind separate from the universe. I don't say that God is proven or the existence of God is proven from the evidence for a beginning, but rather that the postulation of a transcendent mind with the attributes that for example traditional theists have long ascribed to God provides the best, or one of the best, explanations."
The diffference from the Kalam argument seems to be that Meyer is saying its not a proof of the existence of God, but that its "the best, or one of the best explanations", given that the Universe had a beginning.
That, of course, begs the question: What does he mean by the beginning of the Universe? Its by no means clear to me that our concept of time includes its having a beginning: I can't remember my own beginning, let alone the Universe's! So assuming anything about the beginning of time, for example that it happened at a particular moment, is logically problematic.
This is one of physics' greatest mysteries, and it should be treated as such. Thousands of intelligent people have been working on this problem for decades. Assuming anything simple about it is going to lead to trouble.
I don't think I began at a particular moment. I am not a fertilised egg cell, so I didn't begin with the fertilisation. And I'm not a new born baby, so I didn't begin when I was born. This argument can be continued indefinitely for various moments in my becoming.
So I think of myself as continuously evolving. Every new experience changes who I am. And I am (we are all) part of the Universe looking at itself, so that's probably the best, most productive, way to think about the Universe too.
(This idea is very close to Heraclitus'.)
If I didn't begin at a particular moment, then why should the Universe?
Meyer might do better to shift his ground by appealing to his God's transcendence of time. That would allow the Creator to intervene (create) continuously over perceived time, but it would also raise different, possibly insoluble questions — such as: Why don't we perceive objects suddenly popping into existence? Well, maybe we do, but only when using special apparatus, at the quantum level!
ca10:00 to ca11:26 Meyer talks a lot, but says much that feels vague and tendentious. It feels as if he's saying anything he can to support his God hypothesis, and not allowing any rigorous discussion of awkward details. The discussion would have been more productive if Nathan had pinned him down on a few precise details, instead of letting him gloss over the many unclear and misleading passages.
11:26 Nathan: "...you would expect the universe to have not had a beginning at all, that it had this infinity in the past..."
The discussion is floundering:
  1. The Universe need not be infinitely old, even if there is no moment in time when it began (see above). Mathematically, this is just a question of how the time line is defined: Does it contain its starting point or not? There seems no good reason to choose one answer over the other. But whatever the answer is, ambiguity is not the best place to ground an argument for God.
    The problem of the mathematical singularity at the beginning of time, could be solved simply by denying its existence (removing it from the calculation). This reminds me of the way limits were used to put calculus on a sound foundation. Discuss! :-) 
  2. Did God just create the Universe and then leave it to itself? If so, then He is a bad Father/Creator, since He left us to suffer many evils. If not, we might as well just ignore the initial creation, and concentrate on what happened later (which is far more important).
  3. If time existed before the creation of the Universe in the Old Testament, but not in modern physics, what difference does that make to the way we can argue for God?
    The main difference is that we now have to ask what God was doing before the beginning of time. I think the answer to that is quite interesting: We have to say that the timeless, transcendent God invented time itself.
    This matches my belief that God was originally made in the image of Man (and not the other way round). Time and Space are, for me the brain's strategy for reducing complexity. Consciousness is the perception of time and space. As Kastrup says, our brains would melt into an entropic soup if the whole of reality had to be perceived all at once. Time is the prerequisite for perception. Each one of us creates time when we become conscious.
    Interestingly, this relates to the Christian belief in the incarnation — the belief that God became human in the person of Jesus. It seems to me that Jesus' emphasis on love was designed to overcome the problem of evil in the world: God would be a bad Creator/Father if he had just created us and then left us to our own devices in a world full of evils. He needed to become one of us in order to communicate that idea. Love is a consciously experienced (temporal) experience that the timeless God didn't have.
27:02 Meyer returns to the Kalam argument. This time I'd like to take a closer look. He says:
"I'm not arguing in a straightforward Kalam manner deductively. I'm saying [that] if you accept what looks to be the best explanation of the physical reality that as best we can tell the universe had a beginning — from multiple pointers
  1. from observational astronomy
  2. from theoretical physics
  3. both the singularity theorems (which don't completely prove a beginning but they're pointing strongly in that direction asymptotically as you say) or
  4. the Borg-Guth-Vilenkin theorem, same thing, pointing even more strongly because it doesn't have the same restricted or it doesn't have as many conditions on the proof.
Then I think the best explanation is something like a theistic or deistic creator. If you want to circumvent that with one one of these newer infinite universe cosmologies, starting with the quantum cosmological, the quantum cosmology, which is the one that would be most indicated by the reason you can't affirm an absolute singularity is because you have the quantum mechanical conditions that you have to take account of inside the Planck volume. That cosmology and others have theistic implications for other reasons. So it's a kind of there's a robustness to this irrespective of different predicates you end up in the same conclusion and that conclusion is theism."
Objections to this (roughly in order of appearance):
  1. I fail to see how his argument is not deductive. He immediately follows the opening sentence with an "If X then Y" deduction.
    I think he's trying to say that he's applying Cartesian skepsis (or the scientific method) by not claiming that this is a proof of God's existence. But he treats the result as if it were a proof anyway.
  2. Observational astronomy and the Borg-Guth-Vilenkin theorem:
    According to Wikipedia, The singularity predicted by BGV  is [...] not a global singularity, it does not necessarily exist for all possible observers.
    As I noted above, the Universe can have a finite duration, but still not have a singularity at its beginning.
    Why didn't Meyer point that out earlier? Singularities are not important for his argument, so why did he let Nathan go off on a wild goose chase looking for one? Sorry, but this looks like deliberate diversion tactics. Its certainly not standard practice in philosophical discussions.
  3. I agree that the Universe had a beginning, but don't think that a theistic or deistic creator (separate from His creation) is the best explanation. This therefore becomes a matter of opinion. Its not a proper argument.
  4. Which objections, other than the "newer infinite universe cosmologies" has he considered? How did he counter them?
  5. What are the other reasons he mentions, for cosmologies having theological implications? This is all very hand-wavy.
    What does he really mean by a theological implication? What would the consequences be of agreeing to what he means by God? Would we have to worship Him? That may sound alarmist, but I think its only prudent to be aware of the dangers implicit in Meyer's position.
